Rank the provided cat photographs by narrative presence — the degree to which each image implies a story unfolding across time rather than presenting a flat, resolved, static fact. Images with strong narrative presence make the viewer feel that something has just happened, is actively happening, or is about to happen — they create the sensation of walking into the middle of a scene. Look for body language suggesting intention, reaction, or anticipation: ears rotated toward a sound, weight shifted forward before a leap, eyes tracking something outside the frame, a paw extended mid-reach. Look for tension between elements in the scene: a cat and a hand approaching each other, a cat poised at the edge of a surface, a cat watching something move. Even in stillness, narrative presence can exist when the scene implies waiting, watching, or the passage of time — a silhouette in a window at dusk, a cat paused mid-stride. Images that feel complete and resolved — where nothing is about to happen and nothing has just occurred, where the viewer looks, confirms a cat exists, and moves on — should rank lowest. Images that invite the viewer to imagine the moments just before and just after the frame, that feel like one scene pulled from a longer, ongoing story, should rank highest.
