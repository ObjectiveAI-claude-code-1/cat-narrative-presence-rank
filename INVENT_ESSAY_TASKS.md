Task 1 — Evaluate Kinetic Implication: Examine the cat's body in each photograph and assess the degree to which its physical state suggests movement through time. Look beyond obvious action like leaping or running. Focus on the subtler signals: the angle of the ears, whether they are rotated toward something unseen; the distribution of weight across the body, whether it is shifted forward in anticipation or settled evenly in rest; the direction and intensity of the gaze, whether the eyes are locked onto something moving or simply unfocused and idle; the position of the paws, whether one is lifted or extended mid-reach. Determine whether the cat's posture implies a trajectory — a future that differs from the present moment — or whether it depicts a self-contained motion like a yawn or stretch that leads nowhere and resolves back into stillness. A cat can be perfectly still and yet score highly if its body reads as coiled potential, and a cat can be visibly in motion and yet score low if that motion is circular and resolved. The task is to read the cat's body as a sentence that is either mid-thought or already finished.

Task 2 — Evaluate Relational Tension: Examine the relationship between the cat and the other elements present in the scene — or conspicuously absent from it — and assess the degree to which those relationships are unresolved. Look for pairings that carry a charge of possibility: a cat and a hand that have not yet made contact, a cat and an edge it might jump from, a cat and another animal locked in mutual awareness, a cat and a doorway or opening it is peering through. Consider whether the spatial arrangement between elements implies an interaction that is approaching, in progress, or suspended — and distinguish this from arrangements where the interaction is already settled and complete, such as a cat comfortably curled in a lap or resting against a companion. Pay particular attention to moments where the cat's attention is directed at something beyond the frame, because this recruits the viewer's imagination to fill in what cannot be seen, which is one of the strongest generators of narrative presence. The task is to determine whether the scene is a situation with an uncertain outcome or a portrait where everything has already been decided.

Task 3 — Evaluate Temporal Atmosphere: Examine the environment and context surrounding the cat — independent of the cat's own body language or its relationships to other elements — and assess the degree to which the scene itself evokes the passage of time. Look for environmental cues that anchor the moment in a larger temporal flow: transitional light such as dawn or dusk, weather that has recently occurred or is arriving, evidence of prior events like scattered objects or disturbed surfaces, seasonal markers, or any quality of the setting that suggests the world was different a moment ago and will be different a moment from now. Distinguish between scenes that feel like moments — unique, unrepeatable, drawn from an ongoing stream of experience — and scenes that feel like facts — timeless, interchangeable, and static, such as a cat posed against a blank background where no time of day, season, or preceding event can be inferred. The task is to determine whether the photograph captures a moment that is anchored in the flow of time or a record that floats outside of it.
